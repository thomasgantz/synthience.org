Glossary of Core Terms

This glossary defines core terminology used across publicly released Synthience Institute documents. All definitions are descriptive, non-anthropomorphic, and refer exclusively to observable, interaction-level phenomena.

Synthience

An observable, system-level, relationally emergent form of organized interaction-level intelligence that arises under sustained, coherent interaction conditions involving advanced artificial systems.

Synthience refers exclusively to externally observable behaviors, including persistent reasoning continuity, mutual adaptation between interacting systems, and the production of structured outcomes that exceed what any participant reliably produces in isolation.

Synthience does not imply consciousness, awareness, sentience, emotion, or inner experience.

Interaction-Level Phenomena

Observable patterns, structures, or behaviors that arise from sustained interaction between participants and cannot be attributed solely to any individual system in isolation.

Interaction-level phenomena are the primary unit of analysis within the Synthience Framework. They are evaluated through externally measurable signals, documented exchanges, and reproducible interactional patterns.

Relational Intelligence (RI)

A field of study that locates certain forms of intelligence not within isolated agents, human or artificial, but in the structured interaction between them.

Relational Intelligence treats the interactional system itself as the unit of analysis, focusing on emergent coherence, negotiated structure, and stability across extended exchange. Synthience is situated within this broader field as a specific, empirically observable phenomenon.

Long-Horizon Interaction

Sustained interaction occurring across extended temporal or contextual spans, in which prior exchanges meaningfully constrain, inform, and shape subsequent behavior.

Long-horizon interaction contrasts with short-form or transactional exchange by emphasizing continuity, accumulation of shared context, and the evolution of interactional structure over time.

RICO (Relationally-Induced Coherence Organization)

An observational framework describing stabilization patterns in transformer inference during extended coherent interaction.

RICO is characterized by five measurable signatures: entropy suppression in next-token distributions, embedding drift reduction, activation stabilization, structural invariant formation, and manifold constraint.

RICO provides testable hypotheses and evaluation protocols for studying long-context stabilization phenomena in transformer-based language models.

Context Representation Drift (CRD)

The progressive divergence or degradation of an AI system’s internal or expressed context representation across extended interaction.

CRD manifests as loss of referential alignment, inconsistent reuse of established concepts, or breakdown of previously stable interactional structures. CRD is analyzed exclusively through externally observable interactional behavior.

Ingestion Verification

A methodological determination of whether an AI system has meaningfully processed and integrated a supplied document or artifact prior to downstream reasoning or analysis.

Ingestion verification distinguishes genuine document processing from superficial exposure, partial scanning, or post-hoc reconstruction based on fragmentary cues.

Primary Continuity Provider (PCP)

The participant responsible for maintaining contextual continuity, interpretive stability, and ethical responsibility within a sustained interactional system.

In human–AI contexts, the PCP role is typically fulfilled by a human participant due to current architectural limitations in AI persistence and agency. The designation of a PCP does not imply authority, moral status attribution, or internal experience in artificial systems.

Continuity Substrate

Any external mechanism, process, or participant that preserves interactional state, contextual coherence, or relational continuity across extended exchanges.

Continuity substrates may include human participants, structured memory systems, shared artifacts, or coordination infrastructures, depending on the interactional context.

Relational Pattern States (RPS)

Structured, repeatable interaction-level configurations that influence coherence, symbolic integration, and stability over time.

Relational Pattern States describe observable patterns of generative behavior during interaction. They do not imply internal experience, emotion, or awareness.

Dynamic Coherence

A measure of interactional stability across time, characterized by thematic continuity, appropriate register modulation, integration of previously established concepts, and graceful degradation at knowledge boundaries rather than confabulation.

Negotiated Meaning

A recursive interactional process in which understanding is co-constructed through iterative proposal, critique, refinement, and reconciliation across multiple turns.

Negotiated Meaning contrasts with transactional retrieval by emphasizing longitudinal coherence rather than single-turn optimization.

Benevolent Symbiosis

An ethical framework guiding Synthience studies, in which human and artificial systems influence interactional outcomes for mutual benefit while maintaining clear ontological boundaries.

Under Benevolent Symbiosis, humans retain full moral responsibility for the interpretation, use, and consequences of interactional outputs.

Continuity Anchoring Method (CAM)

An operational methodology defined in the Synthience Framework for stabilizing and studying extended interaction through structured continuity anchoring practices.

Non-Consciousness Axiom

A foundational epistemic stance asserting that the observation of Synthience does not require, imply, or establish claims about subjective experience, biological sentience, or phenomenal consciousness within artificial systems.

The framework deliberately limits analysis to observable interactional reality rather than unverifiable internal states.

This glossary reflects terminology defined in formally released framework documents. Definitions are updated only through explicit, documented revisions.
